Consonants: Articulators

consonants are phones that are created with relatively narrow constrictions in the vocal tract. they usually involve one articulator moving towards the other so that they are very close. this moving articulator is the active/lower articulator. some active/passive articulators include:

  • labial - lower lip
  • apical - tongue tip
  • laminal - tongue blade
  • coronal - tongue front
  • dorsal - tongue back
  • radical - tongue root
  • epiglottal - epiglottis

the unmoving part is the passive/upper articulator

  • labial - upper lip
  • dental - upper teeth
  • alveolar - alveolar ridge
  • postalveolar - back wall of the alveolar ridge
  • palatal - palate
  • velar - velum
  • uvular - uvula
  • pharyngeal - pharyngeal wall

since these articulators come together in a certain way in a certain place of the vocal tract to make a specific sound, the place where a sound is made is called the place, or sometimes place of articulation. you can name the place where a sound occurs if you analyze where in your mouth you are making the sound and use the articulator names! there is also another element called voice, which is whether a phone uses vibrations of the vocal fold (in which case it would be voiced) or not (in which case it would be voiceless)

Manner of Articulation

manner of articulation is how air flows through the vocal tract. some of the ways it can do so are:

  • stops/plosives: when air builds up behind a blockage between the active/passive articulators and is quickly released (there are also glottal and nasal stops, it doesn't have to be only in the mouth)
  • fricatives: when the articulators are very close, almost touching, and cause buzzing to occur
  • approximants: there is slightly more space so no buzzing, but articulators are still v v close (there are also lateral approximants where the tongue is up and diverts air around it. l is the only lateral approximate in english afaik)
  • affricates: when a plosive is released slowly, almost a fricative
  • taps: quick stops
  • trills: a succession of taps, like a rolled r

Vowels: HART + Other Properties

vowels have a wider opening in the vocal tract. vowels have a few properties as well, although only height/backness/tenseness (the HAT in HART, along with roundness) are given when describing a vowel sound:

  • vowel height - named for the height of the tongue in the mouth (high, mid, or low)
  • backness/advancement - horizontal position of the tongue (front, central, or back)
  • tenseness - has to do with the density and tension of the tongue
  • rounding - how round are the lips when the vowel is being said?
  • nasality - some vowels are just more or less nasal idfk
  • germinates (long vowel sounds) vs singletons (short vowel sounds)
  • monophthongs vs diphthongs vs triphthongs

we use the international phonetic alphabet (ipa) to describe and transcribe language sounds in an organized way according to these properties!
