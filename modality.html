Modality

modality is the process of communication, specifically the physical nature of how it happens. there's a list thing if it matters:

  1. mental construction: thinking of what you want to communicate
  2. articulation: physically making the sign with your body by saying/signing it
  3. physical linguistic sign: producing the sign
  4. perception: the other party recieving the sign by hearing/seeing it
  5. reconstruction: the other party understanding what you've just told them

when it comes to how language is physically expressed, the most common methods are vocal-auditory and manual-visual. however, there are also manual-tactile languages that usually are used with the deaf-blind.

The Vocal Tract

vocal-auditory languages use the vocal tract to produce language sounds, or phones. the three important regions of the vocal tract are the mouth or oral cavity, the nasal cavity in your nose, and the pharynx or upper throat. the pharynx divides into two parts of the throat: the esophagus and the trachea. the trachea goes to the lungs, so it's the part that is relevant to speech.

also relevant to mention is that english uses pulmonic egression, or breathing out via the lungs to speak. this is the most common airstream mechanism because it produces sound easily, loudly, and efficiently

sounds are produced in sound waves, which are periods of rarification and compression (air builds up behind vocal folds and is released)

pitch comes from the rate at which your vocal folds are opening and closing

loudness comes from the ampitude of the sound waves
